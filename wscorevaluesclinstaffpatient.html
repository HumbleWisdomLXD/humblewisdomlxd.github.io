Crisis Intervention: The Weekend Call

Friday, 4:47 PM - The Call:

You're wrapping up your week when Marcus, a 17-year-old client you've been working with for 3 months, calls in crisis. His voice is shaking, and he's clearly agitated. "I can't do this anymore. Everything's falling apart, and I'm thinking about... you know... ending it all."

Client Background - Marcus, Age 17:

  • Diagnosis: Major Depressive Disorder, Generalized Anxiety
  • History: Two previous suicide attempts (ages 14 and 16)
  • Current stressors: Parents' divorce finalization, college rejection letters, girlfriend broke up with him this week
  • Support system: Lives with mother (works nights), limited peer support
  • Recent progress: Had been showing improvement, using coping strategies, more engaged in school
  • Treatment: Weekly therapy, psychiatrist managing medications
⚠️ CRISIS INDICATORS: Mentions of ending life, emotional dysregulation, multiple recent stressors, history of attempts
Remember: Your decisions should reflect our core values of Client-First Care, Integrity, and Collaboration.

Decision Point 1: Immediate Response

Marcus continues:

"I got another rejection letter today. That's four colleges now. My mom's been crying every night since the divorce papers came. My friends think I'm weird because I don't party anymore. And Sarah... she said I'm 'too intense' and she needs space. I just... I feel like everyone would be better off without me."

You hear him breathing heavily. It's 4:50 PM on Friday. The crisis hotline is available, but you know Marcus struggles with talking to strangers.

Question 1: What's your immediate therapeutic response?

A) "Marcus, I'm going to connect you with the crisis hotline right now. They're trained for these situations and can help you through the weekend. I'll see you Monday."
B) "I hear how much pain you're in right now. Let's slow this down together. Can you tell me what's happening in your body right now? Are you safe where you are?"
C) "This sounds really serious. I think we need to get you to the emergency room right away. Can you call your mom to take you, or should I call emergency services?"
D) "I understand you're upset, but remember the coping strategies we've worked on. Try the breathing exercises and call me back if you're still feeling this way."

Decision Point 2: Assessment and Planning

Marcus responds positively to your initial intervention:

"Yeah, I'm at home. Mom's at work until midnight. I'm in my room... I keep looking at that bottle of pills from when I was really bad before. I haven't touched them, but they're right there on my desk. I know I was doing better, but this week just... everything hit at once."

Clinical Assessment: Client has means present, expressing suicidal ideation, but shows some protective factors (calling you, acknowledging previous progress). Ambivalence present.

Question 2: How do you approach the risk assessment and safety planning?

A) Complete a formal suicide risk assessment over the phone, document everything thoroughly, and follow standard protocol for high-risk clients.
B) Ask Marcus to remove the pills from his room while you stay on the phone, then work together to identify people he can contact and activities that might help him through the weekend.
C) Focus on validating his feelings and exploring the specific triggers from this week, helping him process the emotions before addressing safety.
D) Immediately contact his mother at work and his psychiatrist to coordinate a comprehensive safety response with his full treatment team.

Decision Point 3: Follow-up and Continuity

The situation stabilizes:

Marcus has removed the pills and put them in the kitchen. He's called his older brother who's coming over. His breathing has slowed, and he's able to identify some things that give him hope. He says, "I think I'm okay for tonight. But I'm scared about feeling this bad again. What if you're not available next time?"

Current status: Immediate risk reduced, support person coming, client expressing concern about future episodes - shows good insight and motivation for safety planning.

Question 3: How do you ensure continuity of care and long-term safety?

A) Schedule an emergency session for tomorrow (Saturday) and arrange for daily check-ins over the weekend to ensure close monitoring.
B) Create a detailed safety plan together, schedule a session for early next week, and coordinate with his psychiatrist and mother about the crisis and ongoing support.
C) Document the crisis thoroughly, provide him with crisis resources, and plan to discuss this extensively in your regular session next week.
D) Recommend intensive outpatient programming or higher level of care to ensure he has more support available during vulnerable times.

Clinical Excellence in Action

Values in Clinical Practice:

Your decisions demonstrated how our core values guide effective clinical care:

  • Client-First Care: Staying present with Marcus in his crisis moment, prioritizing his immediate needs
  • Integrity: Honest assessment of risk while building on his strengths and autonomy
  • Collaboration: Working with Marcus, his family, and treatment team for comprehensive support

Three Months Later:

Marcus successfully navigated that crisis weekend and several smaller challenges since then. He was accepted to his safety school, developed stronger coping strategies, and rebuilt his relationship with his girlfriend. Most importantly, he now has a robust safety plan and knows how to access help.

Just like the psychologist from our opening story, your value-driven approach in that critical 4:47 PM moment made the difference between a client thriving and a potentially tragic outcome.
